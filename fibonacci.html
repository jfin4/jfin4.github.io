Fibonacci Branching Patterns

Figure 1. An arrangement of Fibonacci branching patterns. The basic motif is the circular branching pattern, which is repeatedly inverteted and superimposed at various scales. The branching pattern was inspired by the Fibonacci sequence—the pattern branches at regular intervals and the number of nodes at each interval follows the Fibonacci sequece. That is to say, the number of nodes at each interval is the sum of the number of nodes at the previous two intervals. This arrangement was created using R.

I am fascinated by branching structures, they seem to show up just about everywhere. Plants are obvious examples. Every plant, from redwood to cryptantha, has a signature branching structure that allows immediate identification by a trained eye. Watersheds and cardiovascular systems are other examples. Branching patterns extend through time as readily as through space. Family trees are so named for their resemblance to their wooden counterparts when sketched onto paper, but that sketch is a spatial representation of a temporal process. We come into being as microscopic nodes embedded in the uterine lining of our mothers. There we are nourished until we grow too large to remain attached. An alien eye would find our resulting form indistinguishable from that of our progenitors. This self–similarity is another fascinating aspect of branching structures.
